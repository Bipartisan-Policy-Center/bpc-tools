300% AMI (proxy)

The FSTC program uses 300% of HUD Area Median Income, which varies by metropolitan area and county — there is no single statewide number. No pre-built state-level dataset exists for this threshold. This calculator uses the Census ACS share of K–12 public school children in households below 300% of the federal poverty line as a conservative proxy (ACS Table B17024 / NCES ACS-ED). Because the poverty line is lower than AMI in most areas, this proxy still understates the true AMI-eligible population.
FRPL / Economically Disadvantaged

FRPL covers households at or below 185% of the federal poverty level (NCES CCD 2022-23). Because the FSTC program's eligibility threshold is 300% of HUD Area Median Income — which is higher than the poverty line in most areas — FRPL is a lower-bound proxy that understates the true eligible population. True eligible populations will be larger than this estimate.

Enrollment NCES Common Core of Data, Table 203.20 — public K–12 enrollment by state, Fall 2023.
Scholarship cost Research range of $1,200–$2,500 per student per year for in-person high-impact tutoring. Source: Kraft, Matthew A., Brown University.
Method A: FRPL eligibility NCES CCD 2022-23 state FRPL rates. Covers households at or below 185% of the federal poverty level. Because FRPL's threshold (185% poverty) is below the program's 300% AMI standard, this is a lower-bound proxy — true eligible populations will be larger. FRPL rates may further undercount disadvantage in states with Community Eligibility Provision (CEP) programs.
Method B: 300% AMI proxy The FSTC program uses 300% of HUD Area Median Income, which varies by county and family size — no single statewide figure exists. This calculator uses Census ACS 5-year estimates of school-age children (ages 5–17) in households below 300% of the federal poverty line as a conservative proxy (ACS Table B17024 / NCES ACS-ED). Because the poverty line is lower than AMI in most areas, this also understates true eligibility.
Method C: Percent of eligible taxpayers Sets the student population from the donor side: a given percentage of eligible taxpayers each contribute $1,700, generating a total fund (contributors × $1,700), from which the number of students served is derived (⌊ total funds ÷ scholarship cost ⌋). Federal FSTC · §25F

Qualified Educational Expenses

Scholarships may be used for the following categories of expenses under the federal Education Freedom Tax Credit program.

Category 1 — Instruction & Academic Support
🏫
Tuition & Fees School tuition and enrollment fees.
📚
Academic Tutoring Tutoring services directly supporting academic achievement.
🧩
Special Needs Services Services required for special needs students in connection with school enrollment or attendance.
📖
Books, Supplies & Equipment Books, supplies, and other equipment expenses incurred in connection with school enrollment or attendance.
Category 2 — School-Required Supplementary Items
🏠
Room & Board Required or provided by a school in connection with enrollment or attendance.
👕
Uniforms Required or provided by a school in connection with enrollment or attendance.
🚌
Transportation Required or provided by a school in connection with enrollment or attendance.
🕔
Extended Day Programs & Supplementary Services Including extended day programs which are required or provided by a school in connection with enrollment or attendance.
Category 3 — Technology & Internet Access
💻
Computer Technology or Equipment If used by the student and the student's family during any of the years the student is in school.
🌐
Internet Access & Related Services If used by the student and the student's family during any of the years the student is in school.
Not included: Computer software designed for sports, games, or hobbies that are not predominantly educational in nature does not qualify as an eligible expense.
Federal FSTC · §25F · Guidance Note

$1,700 Credit Limit: Per Return, Not Per Person

An important interpretation note on the maximum donor contribution.

⚠️
Anticipated guidance on married filing jointly Calculator assumes a limit of $1,700 per return, which means that married-filing-jointly couples would receive $1,700, not $3,400. Final Treasury rules could impact this.
Implication for this calculator: All donor and program-size estimates in this tool assume $1,700 per contributor, consistent with the anticipated per-return interpretation. If guidance ultimately permits $3,400 for joint filers, the number of donors required to fund a given program size would be lower than shown here — meaning these estimates are conservative under that alternative interpretation.
